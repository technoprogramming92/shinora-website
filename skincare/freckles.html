Description

Tiny, reddish-brown dots on your skin are called freckles.

They are typically observed on parts of your body that are exposed to the sun, such as your face, neck, back, upper chest, hands, and arms.

Although freckles are normally benign, it is always advisable to monitor them for any changes.

Freckle-fading treatments include cryotherapy, lasers, and chemical and topical peels.

Types of Freckles

There are two types of freckles: ephelides and solar lentigines.

Ephelides are flat and typically red or tan-to-brown in color. They are commonly associated with sun exposure and sunburns, often appearing on areas of the body exposed to sunlight such as the face, arms, upper chest, neck, and back. Ephelides are more prevalent in individuals with lighter hair and skin tones, particularly those of Caucasian and Asian descent. They usually first appear in childhood and continue to develop into young adulthood, gradually fading with age.

Solar lentigines, also known as actinic lentigines, liver spots, or age spots, present as yellow to red to brown to dark patches of skin. They primarily affect adults over the age of 40 and are attributed to repeated sun exposure over time. These spots commonly appear on sun-exposed areas like the face, forearms, back of hands, chest, back, shoulders, and lower legs.

How to Prepare for Freckles?

Before undergoing freckles treatment, it's essential to take certain precautions to ensure optimal results and minimize the risk of complications. Firstly, it's important to avoid sun exposure as much as possible and use a broad-spectrum sunscreen with a high SPF daily. Sun protection helps prevent further darkening of existing freckles and reduces the likelihood of developing new ones.

If you have any existing skin conditions or allergies, it's crucial to inform your dermatologist before undergoing freckles treatment. This information will help your dermatologist tailor the treatment plan to your specific needs and minimize the risk of adverse reactions.

What to Expect During Freckles treatment?

Since freckles are normally harmless, there's usually no need for treatment. However, if you're prone to freckles or want to stop new freckles from forming, it's crucial to limit your exposure to the sun and wear sunscreen every day.

Although freckles cannot be totally removed, there are a number of ways to lessen them, including topical treatments, lasers, and cryotherapy.

You can apply products that include acids, such as glycolic acid, phenol, trichloracetic acid, or alpha hydroxy acid, to encourage the shedding of the outermost layer of skin cells and to stimulate the creation of new ones.

The creation of collagen and the turnover of skin cells are promoted by retinoids and retinols, which helps to lighten freckles.

Freckles can be effectively removed from the top layer of skin with lasers.

In order to darken and finally lose freckles, cryotherapy involves freezing them using liquid nitrogen.
