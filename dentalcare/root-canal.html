What Is Root Canal Treatment?

Treatment Details

How it's done?
Infections of the tooth pulp, which is your teeth's innermost layer, can be treated by a root canal treatment.
Benefits:
Relief, Restoration, Preservation

Description

For infections in the tooth pulp, which is the innermost layer of your teeth, a root canal is the recommended course of action.

If bacteria in your mouth find their way into the pulp of your teeth and inflame it, you might require a root canal (pulpitis). Bacterial attack could target the pulp of your teeth if:

deep cavity is there in your tooth or Plaque bacteria have infected the pulp of your tooth, causing it to become broken or damaged.

How to Prepare for Root Canal treatment?

Dental X-rays of the problematic tooth will be taken by your dentist or endodontist prior to starting therapy to ensure whether your tooth pulp is inflamed, diseased, or dead and to confirm that you will require a root canal.

Follow any pre-care instructions provided, like avoiding eating or drinking before your appointment, Maintain good oral hygiene by brushing and flossing regularly.

What to Expect During Root Canal Treatment?

Your dentist administers anesthesia to numb your infected tooth and the surrounding gum area. If you experience dental anxiety, your dentist may also prescribe medications to help you relax. However, these medications may induce drowsiness, so it's important not to drive immediately following your treatment.

Once the area is numb and you're comfortable, your dentist places a thin, flexible piece of rubber, known as a dental dam, over the infected tooth and nearby gums. This dental dam serves to keep your tooth dry during the procedure, ensuring optimal conditions for treatment.

Using specialized dental instruments, your dentist drills a small hole in the crown of your tooth, providing access to the inner pulp chamber. This step is crucial for reaching and addressing the infected or damaged pulp tissue within your tooth.

With the pulp chamber now accessible, your dentist carefully removes the infected or damaged nerves, blood vessels, and tissues from inside the tooth pulp. This process effectively clears out the diseased tissue, helping to alleviate pain and prevent further infection.

Following the removal of the pulp, your dentist thoroughly cleans and disinfects the pulp chamber and root canals. This ensures that all bacteria and debris are effectively eliminated, reducing the risk of reinfection.

Once the pulp chamber and root canals are cleaned and disinfected, your dentist fills them with a flexible, rubbery material called gutta-percha. This material helps to seal off the empty spaces, preventing bacteria from re-entering the tooth and causing further issues.

After filling the pulp chamber and root canals, your dentist seals the access hole in the crown of your tooth with a temporary dental filling. This temporary seal provides temporary protection against bacteria while awaiting the final restoration.

In the final step, your dentist places a dental crown over the treated tooth. Dental crowns are custom-made to fit your tooth precisely and provide protection and stability. However, the fabrication of the crown may take some time, typically two to three weeks, before it can be placed on your tooth.
