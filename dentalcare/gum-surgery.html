What Is Gum Surgery?

Treatment Details

How it's done?
Treatment options for gum disease vary according on the illness's stage, your dental health, and general well-being. Surgeons can choose between guided tissue regeneration and pocket reduction surgery.
Benefits:
Protection, Restoration, Functionality

Description

Gum disease that causes significant inflammation is called periodontitis, and symptoms include red, bleeding, or swollen gums.

Plaque and tartar accumulation on your teeth's surfaces leads to gum disease development. Your gums swell, bleed, and become sensitive in response to the germs in these irritants. The act of brushing or flossing may sometimes cause bleeding gums.

You have a higher chance of maintaining long-term dental health the sooner you treat gum disease. Gum disease is reversible in its early stage, known as gingivitis.

How to Prepare for Gum Surgery?

Preparing for gum surgery involves scheduling a consultation with your periodontist to discuss the procedure and address any concerns. Follow any specific instructions provided, such as fasting or adjusting medications.

Your dentist will do a thorough examination of your gums, teeth, and supporting structures during this session. This may involve taking X-rays or other diagnostic inspection.

First, make an appointment for a consultation to go over your choices for treatment and have the severity of your periodontal disease evaluated by your dentist or periodontist.

What to Expect During Gum Surgery?

The Gum Surgery may include treatements varies upon recommandation by your health care provider. These are described below:

Pocket reduction surgery, also known as flap surgery, involves incisions along the gum line to temporarily lift the gums away from the teeth. This allows access to the roots for thorough cleaning of tartar buildup and root surfaces. Additionally, damaged bone areas may be smoothed and reshaped to hinder bacterial growth. Finally, the gums are repositioned and sutured back into place to restore gum health.

Bone grafting procedures utilize your own bone, donated bone, or synthetic bone to rebuild areas damaged by gum disease. Acting as scaffolding, the graft supports space until new bone can regenerate. Often performed alongside pocket reduction surgery, bone grafting aids in restoring bone structure and supporting tooth stability.

Gum grafting addresses gum recession, a common symptom of periodontal disease, by using your own tissue, donated tissue, or synthetic tissue to treat affected areas. During the surgery, tissue grafts are placed over receded gums and sutured into position, with the option of using tissue from the roof of the mouth.

Guided tissue regeneration involves placing a membrane in areas where gaps have formed between the tooth root and bone due to periodontal disease. This membrane prevents gum tissue from growing into the area where bone should be, allowing the body time to regenerate bone around the tooth. Often, bone grafts are also placed during this procedure to support bone regeneration.
